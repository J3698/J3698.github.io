Electric Longboard: Attaching Lights

Written 7/15/2018
Edited 7/16/2018
Out of all of the bits and pieces of this electric longboard project, working with lights has been one of the most difficult and exciting parts. Disclaimer, yes my friend shihao did the mechanical assembly for the motor, but still - a lot of longboards have nice motors, not a lot of longboard have wicked lights. So let's dive in. I've included a list of parts at the end.

Light Fixture #0 For my first attempt at attaching lights to the longboard, I cut out small squares of wood using my highschool's bandsaw. Then I hot glued the squares onto the longboard, and the lights onto the squares. At first it worked out... but the hot glue connections would suddenly come loose.


Light Fixture #1 Hot glue wasn't working, so I turned to using wood screws. I would drill a starter hole all the way through the wood and then longboard, and drill the wood screws in after. Sometimes the wood screw would go all the way through the longboard, making for a dangerous point that came up through the grip tape, so I ground the points down with a dremel.
Instead of squares, I used longer pieces of wood for the straight parts of the fixture - they provided more surface area to connect the lights to. The most annoying part here was taking the trucks off to screw in the blocks of wood on the far ends of the board.


Applying Velcro Cutting velcro was the messiest part of the light fixture. The industrial strength adhesive catches in scissors, so after one cut, gunk collects on the cutting tool. I used a pair of Cutco Super Shears to start each cut of the Velcro, but did the rest of the cut with an X-Acto knife. Before putting velcro on the wooden blocks, I cleaned the blocks with rubbing alcohol and let them dry.

There are two parts to velcro. The fuzzy part is called the "loop" side - it resembles loops on a carpet. The stiffer side is called the "hook" side. Generally the loops pick up trash more easily, and need to be replaced more often - so they are put on the side that is easier to replace. For some reason I thought it would be easier to replace the velcro on the lights, so I put the loops on the lights, and the hooks on the board.
After that, I was done! Next it was time to perfect the light programming


Parts
  • Wood (~1/2" thick) - This is for mounting the lights. I got a bunch of wood for free from a scrap pile at school, and then borrowed some more from my sister - I'd bought her some earlier for $10 to play around with the jigsaw and sander.
  • LED RGB Strip - Addressable, Sealed (1m) (2x) - I got these from sparkfun for $50 total. These are WS2812s, with a wire for ground, power, and data. LEDs with two data wires (5060 LEDs) will probably play better with other arduino accessories, but check the reviews on durability first, and decide if you want your lights to be addressable (i.e do you want cool patterns, i.e. yes).
  • 1" Self-Drilling Wood-Screws - To connect the light fixtures to the board! I got 100 of these from home depoot for $8, although I can't find them on the website. It's okay if the screws aren't self-drilling.
  • 4 ft. x 2 in. Industrial Strength Velcro - This stuff is great, so the $10 is worth being able to safely attach and detach your $50 worth of ligts.
Parts Total: ~$78

Tools
  • Jig Saw - A bandsaw or even handsaw will work, but you need to be able to cut 1/2" thickwood, so I got a hypertough jigsaw from walmart for only $20.
  • Electric Drill - If you can drill by hand, congratulations - you'll be connecting 1/2" thick wood together. Again I went with hypertough, it was the cheapest I saw at walmart, at $20. Mine is corded, as I don't think I'll have use for a cordless drill any time soon.
  • Drill Bit Set - I only ended up using one bit to drill starter holes, and the 1/4" hex bit, but I'm sure the 109 piece drill bit set I got for $18 will come in handy again.
Tools Total: ~$58
